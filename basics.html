Stock Investing Basics

Start here if you're new to stocks. We'll cover the fundamentals in simple terms—no jargon overload.

What Is a Stock?

A stock (also called equity or share) represents partial ownership in a company. When you buy a stock, you become a shareholder and own a tiny piece of that business. If the company does well (profits grow, etc.), the stock price usually rises, and you can sell for a profit.

Companies issue stocks to raise money for growth. In return, shareholders may get dividends (a share of profits) and voting rights on big decisions.

Why Do People Invest in Stocks?

  • Growth potential: Stocks historically return more than savings accounts or bonds over long periods (though with more risk).
  • Dividends: Some companies pay regular cash to shareholders.
  • Ownership: You're part-owner of real businesses (e.g., Apple, Coca-Cola).
  • Inflation protection: Stocks tend to grow faster than inflation.

Key Stock Terms for Beginners

Dividend

A portion of company profits paid to shareholders (usually quarterly). Not all stocks pay dividends—growth companies often reinvest profits instead.

P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings)

Stock price divided by earnings per share. Low P/E may mean undervalued; high P/E often means high growth expectations.

Bull Market vs. Bear Market

Bull = rising prices (optimism); Bear = falling prices (pessimism). Markets go up and down—long-term trend is up.

Diversification

Spreading investments across different stocks/sectors to reduce risk. "Don't put all eggs in one basket."

Types of Stocks

  • Common Stock: Most common type—voting rights + potential dividends.
  • Preferred Stock: Fixed dividends, priority over common in payouts, but usually no voting rights.
  • Growth Stocks: Companies expected to grow fast (e.g., tech)—high P/E, low/no dividends.
  • Value Stocks: Undervalued based on fundamentals (low P/E)—often pay dividends.
